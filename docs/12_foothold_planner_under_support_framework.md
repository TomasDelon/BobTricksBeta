# 12 — Foothold planner under the support-centered framework

In the `CM-first` procedural locomotion architecture, the foothold planner must no longer be understood as an isolated module that chooses a single ankle point in front of the body. Once standing, gait initiation, support progression, and load acceptance are modeled explicitly, the planner must be interpreted as a support-acquisition planner. Its task is to propose a future contact that can be geometrically realized by the swing foot, dynamically accepted by the support system, and inserted into the same support-centered state architecture without breaking continuity.

The future foothold is still anchored by a future ankle point

$$
A_{\mathrm{next}} \in \Gamma,
$$

but the planner output must now be richer than the ankle point alone. The minimum planner output is the tuple

$$
\Pi_{\mathrm{foot}} = \left(A_{\mathrm{next}}, \mathcal{W}_{\mathrm{td}}, \mathcal{V}_{\mathrm{td}}\right),
$$

where $\mathcal{W}_{\mathrm{td}}$ is a candidate touchdown window and $\mathcal{V}_{\mathrm{td}}$ is the viability certificate associated with that candidate. The ankle remains the geometric anchor, but the planner now proposes a support-acquisition event, not merely a target point in world space.

The search domain must remain terrain-following and facing-consistent. Let the current dominant support ankle be $A_{\mathrm{st}}$ and let the terrain forward path in the current facing direction be parameterized by arc-length as

$$
\gamma_{A_{\mathrm{st}}}(\ell), \qquad \ell \ge 0.
$$

Every future ankle candidate is of the form

$$
A(\ell) = \gamma_{A_{\mathrm{st}}}(\ell), \qquad \ell \in \Sigma_{\mathrm{plan}}.
$$

The search interval $\Sigma_{\mathrm{plan}}$ must be finite and state-dependent. A purely fixed search horizon is not acceptable as the canonical law because terrain compensation and reactive stepping require the horizon to deform with the current locomotion state. The correct support-centered interpretation is that the search horizon depends on the support regime, the progression state, and the reactive intensity. One therefore defines

$$
\Sigma_{\mathrm{plan}} = [\ell_{\min}^{\mathrm{plan}}, \ell_{\max}^{\mathrm{plan}}(\rho, \mathcal{M}, X)].
$$

The lower bound excludes degenerate tiny steps. The upper bound is allowed to grow with reactivity and, if needed, with protocol-specific anticipation.

A future ankle candidate is not admissible merely because it lies on the terrain. It must induce a full future support geometry. For each candidate $A_{\mathrm{next}}$, define the local future contact path under the foot

$$
\chi_{\mathrm{next}}(\sigma), \qquad \sigma \in [-L_{\mathrm{heel}}, L_{\mathrm{toe}}],
$$

with

$$
\chi_{\mathrm{next}}(0) = A_{\mathrm{next}}.
$$

The candidate foot geometry is admissible only if the full support path exists over the active heel-to-toe interval, if the path spans at most two admissible consecutive terrain segments, if the angle variation along the active contact path remains below the allowed foot-angle jump threshold, and if the resulting foot direction remains facing-compatible. The future support point is not yet uniquely fixed at planning time because it depends on the future support coordinate, but the planner must at least certify that a valid support path exists and that a valid future support frame can be constructed from it.

The planner must then evaluate the candidate using candidate-conditioned prediction. Let the current locomotion state be $X_0$. For each candidate foothold, define the candidate-conditioned hybrid prediction

$$
X_{\mathrm{td}}(A_{\mathrm{next}}) = \Pi\bigl(t_{\mathrm{td}}(A_{\mathrm{next}}); X_0, A_{\mathrm{next}}\bigr),
$$

where the touchdown time is itself generated by the candidate-conditioned swing and support evolution. This prediction must propagate the active support state, the gait-initiation or walking mode, the swing geometry, and the upcoming support-acquisition phase. If the predictor cannot produce a geometrically coherent touchdown state, then the candidate must be rejected as non-viable.

The future pelvis state remains central. The predicted pelvis at touchdown is

$$
P_{\mathrm{td}} = C_{\mathrm{td}} - 0.75L e_{\theta_{\mathrm{td}}}.
$$

The future candidate must satisfy exact rigid-leg reachability

$$
\|P_{\mathrm{td}} - A_{\mathrm{next}}\| \le 2L.
$$

This is a hard constraint. It is not softened by reactivity or by planner preference. The planner may prefer some touchdown geometries over others, but no candidate violating exact rigid morphology may remain in the admissible set.

The planner must also reason in terms of future support acceptance, not only immediate touchdown. Let the current dominant support foot be indexed by $b$ and the candidate new support foot by $f$. Then the planner must anticipate not only a touchdown pose, but the possibility of a finite load-acceptance phase from the old support toward the new one. A candidate is therefore acceptable only if the predicted state admits a valid sequence

$$
\text{swing realization} \to \text{touchdown} \to \text{load acceptance} \to \text{new support dominance}.
$$

This changes the meaning of step viability. A candidate that is reachable at the exact touchdown instant but cannot pass through a valid bilateral acceptance phase must still be rejected.

The local geometry needed for support-centered scoring must be defined relative to the future support state. Suppose the candidate-conditioned prediction returns a valid future support path and a support coordinate initialization for the contacting foot. Then the future support point is

$$
Q_{\mathrm{next}} = \chi_{\mathrm{next}}(\sigma_{\mathrm{next}}),
$$

and the future support tangent is

$$
t_{\mathrm{sup,next}} = \frac{\partial_{\sigma} \chi_{\mathrm{next}}(\sigma_{\mathrm{next}})}{\|\partial_{\sigma} \chi_{\mathrm{next}}(\sigma_{\mathrm{next}})\|}.
$$

The future support normal is

$$
n_{\mathrm{sup,next}} = (-t_{{\mathrm{sup,next}},y}, t_{{\mathrm{sup,next}},x}).
$$

The predicted CM is then decomposed in the future support frame as

$$
C_{\mathrm{td}} = Q_{\mathrm{next}} + s_{Q,\mathrm{next}} t_{\mathrm{sup,next}} + z_{Q,\mathrm{next}} n_{\mathrm{sup,next}}.
$$

Likewise, relative to the future ankle,

$$
C_{\mathrm{td}} = A_{\mathrm{next}} + s_{A,\mathrm{next}} t_{\mathrm{sup,next}} + z_{A,\mathrm{next}} n_{\mathrm{sup,next}}.
$$

The ankle-local coordinate remains important because the future support coordinate is still a separate variable. One has

$$
s_{Q,\mathrm{next}} = s_{A,\mathrm{next}} - \sigma_{\mathrm{next}}.
$$

This means that future support scoring must remain support-centered but ankle-aware.

The planner must use a capture-related quantity, but not as its sole law. A support-centered XCoM-like quantity may be defined at touchdown by

$$
\xi_{\mathrm{next}} = s_{Q,\mathrm{next}} + \lambda \frac{\dot s_{A,\mathrm{next}}}{\omega_{0,\mathrm{next}}},
$$

where

$$
\dot s_{A,\mathrm{next}} = \dot C_{\mathrm{td}} \cdot t_{\mathrm{sup,next}}
$$

and

$$
\omega_{0,\mathrm{next}} = \sqrt{\frac{g \cos \alpha_{\mathrm{sup,next}}}{\bar z_{\mathrm{next}}}}.
$$

This quantity is useful because it estimates whether the predicted state can be locally captured by the future support. But it must remain only one term among others. The support-centered planner must also evaluate the support-acceptance geometry, the support progression implied after touchdown, the timing of load acceptance, and the continuity of the support state.

The natural step-length variable remains terrain arc-length. Define the candidate terrain length by

$$
\ell(A_{\mathrm{next}})
$$

measured along the terrain path from the current dominant support ankle to the candidate ankle. The planner should prefer a protocol-dependent target step length

$$
\ell^{\star}_{\mathrm{step}}(X, \rho, \mathcal{M})
$$

rather than a fixed value. The target may depend on whether the system is initiating gait from standing, already in walking, or strongly reactive. This is the correct place for anticipation and support adaptation to influence step size.

The planner must also encode a terrain-compensation objective. The literature on uneven terrain suggests that compensation should not be purely reactive at the instant of contact. Small up-steps and down-steps are more economically traversed when body momentum is modulated in advance. In the present support-centered architecture, the equivalent design principle is that a candidate foothold should be scored not only by whether it is statically admissible, but by whether it reduces the future support and momentum compensation burden. Therefore, terrain height change and terrain-angle change are not merely geometric penalties. They are proxies for future support and momentum regulation cost.

Define the candidate height change by

$$
\Delta h(A_{\mathrm{next}}) = (A_{\mathrm{next}} - A_{\mathrm{st}}) \cdot e_Y.
$$

Define the candidate support-angle change by

$$
\Delta \alpha(A_{\mathrm{next}}) = \alpha_{\mathrm{sup,next}} - \alpha_{\mathrm{sup,current}}.
$$

These quantities may then enter the planner cost as support-transition difficulty measures.

The planner must also remain consistent with gait initiation. If the current protocol is standing or gait initiation, the first planned step must be compatible with the support-unloading process described in the previous working document. In particular, the planner must not choose a future swing foot independently of the gait-initiation support reorganization. The initial swing-side choice and the foothold choice are therefore coupled. If the gait-initiation phase has already selected the future swing foot, then the foothold search must be restricted to that side. If the gait-initiation phase is not yet committed, then side choice and foothold choice should be optimized jointly or by a clear sequential logic.

A candidate foothold is therefore admissible only if all the following hold. It lies in the terrain-forward search set. It induces an admissible future contact path over the full heel-to-toe interval. The candidate-conditioned prediction yields a coherent touchdown state. Exact rigid-leg reachability is satisfied. A finite load-acceptance phase is predicted to be viable. The side choice is compatible with the current gait-initiation or walking support state. If any of these conditions fails, the candidate must be rejected before scoring.

Among admissible candidates, the planner cost must remain support-centered. A canonical form is

$$
J(A_{\mathrm{next}}) = w_{\mathrm{cap}} D_{\mathrm{cap}} + w_{\ell} D_{\ell} + w_h D_h + w_{\alpha} D_{\alpha} + w_{\mathrm{acc}} D_{\mathrm{acc}} + w_t D_t + w_{\mathrm{IK}} D_{\mathrm{IK}}.
$$

Here $D_{\mathrm{cap}}$ is the future support-capture defect, $D_{\ell}$ is the deviation from the target step length, $D_h$ is the terrain height-change cost, $D_{\alpha}$ is the support-angle change cost, $D_{\mathrm{acc}}$ is the predicted load-acceptance difficulty, $D_t$ is the timing mismatch cost, and $D_{\mathrm{IK}}$ is a soft penalty associated with approaching the rigid-leg limit even while remaining inside it.

The new term $D_{\mathrm{acc}}$ is essential in the support-centered framework. It measures how difficult it will be to pass from old support dominance to new support dominance after touchdown. A candidate may be geometrically reachable yet still produce a poor acceptance phase because the bilateral support points are badly arranged, because the support-angle discontinuity is too high, or because the required progression of $\beta$ would be too abrupt. Such a candidate must be scored poorly even if its immediate touchdown looks acceptable.

The planner must also remain protocol-aware. In standing-to-walk transition, the first step should be chosen to help the system exit bilateral standing cleanly. In nominal walking, the next step should preserve natural support progression and momentum flow. In reactive conditions, the cost weights and search horizon may deform with $\rho$, but the hard constraints remain unchanged. In particular, reactivity may enlarge the horizon or shift the target step length, but it may never legalize a candidate that violates exact morphology or admissible foot contact geometry.

The correct procedural interpretation is now clear. The planner no longer asks only where the ankle should land. It asks which future contact acquisition best continues the support-centered locomotion process. The selected step must be reachable by the swing foot, support-admissible under the future foot, acceptable through a finite load-transfer phase, and compatible with the future support evolution of the `CM-first` state. This is the correct planner architecture for human-like procedural locomotion over hills and uneven piecewise-linear terrain, and it keeps the same state backbone that will later be reused by running, crouching, and jumping.
